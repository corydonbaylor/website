BUENOS AIRES

I remember sitting down in a little cafe. It felt very bohemian, rustic even. It had nice wifi, and as I drank a cup of tea (I have a weak stomach, so I don't drink coffee), I read the news on my phone, feeling like in this San Telmo neighborhood of Buenos Aires, I had finally discovered the little hipster, bohemian enclave that I had hoped to find.

I could see this as home.

This is one of two potential locations where I suspect I got the worst food poisoning I had ever experienced in my life. The little bit of milk that I had put in my tea was the prime suspect, the other being a glass of orange juice I had ordered at the airport. I shall never know what did the deed. It is one of life's great mysterious.

I had lost my only credit card the night before. I had left it in the cab that had taken me from the port to my hostel. There is no more disconcerting feeling than finding yourself suddenly disconnected from the financial system in a foreign country when traveling alone. You become hyperaware of how little cash you have on hand and even more aware of how much everything costs.

That play money feeling you get when abroad is gone.

You feel yourself searching for a reset button that doesn't exist. You are suddenly and nearly unredeemablely screwed. But redemption does exist, and it comes in the form of Western Union. Without my mother (aren't moms the best?) sending me money, I may very well have had to wander the streets of Buenos Aires for the next week, cashless, while I waited for my flight home.

That feeling of disconnect pervades Buenos Aires as a whole. There are few cities that prompt the question of why does this place even exist at all?

It’s not surrounded by sea and hills, like Hong Kong and Nagasaki, nor does it lie on a trade route along which civilization has navigated for centuries, like London, Paris, Florence, Geneva, Prague and Vienna. No traveler arrives in Buenos Aires en route to somewhere else. Beyond the city there is no somewhere else

From "The Tango Singer" by Tomás Eloy Martínez

Buenos Aires is a bizarre place. The architecture is French; the people, Italian; and the language is Spanish, and all of it is positively in the middle of nowhere. I'm sure that many Brazilians and Uruguayans would disagree, but outside of Montevideo, which feels like the younger, hippy brother of Buenos Aires, there are no great surrounding cities. There is no where to take the train or bus to. You are already at your destination.

Tucked into the cold arid end of the southern part of the South American continent, there lies a turn-of-the-century South American Paris, frozen in time with it's resplendent beaux-art homes and gracious wide avenues.

And it really does look like Paris, but it looks like Paris if things had gone wrong somehow. A Paris where perhaps the Soviets had taken over, and everything became a bit more drab and worn. Or a Paris where the people couldn't afford to stop the brutalist architectural invasion of the 1950s. Or a Paris where dictators and populists stripped the city of its wealth through bribes and corruption.

And in many ways, Buenos Aires is that. The city was one of the richest in the world in until about the Great Depression, after which it swan dived into the middle income trap.

Perhaps it was Peron, that proto-fascist-socialist-populist who has defined the past twentieth century in the country, who is the reason Buenos Aires is not considered one of the great cities of the world.

My suspicion is that Argentina never properly industrialized. The country remains big in agribusiness. It eats the most beef per capita, as the tour guides will surely tell you. But farming isn't the path to enrichment anymore, as it was at the turn of the century. And as the tour guides will also tell you, somewhat dryly, Buenos Aires has already seen its best days.

TERMINALS AND TAXIS

"Do you need taxi?", said a voice behind me. I turned around to see a short Argentine man with a mustache.

I had just arrived in Argentina and was exhausted. I can never sleep on flights and like seemingly every international flight, mine was a red eye. Customs was as bad as I had ever seen it, taking almost two hours for someone to stamp my passport. The taxi stand only took cash, and there was only two ATMs. One was broken and the other had a line that wrapped around the hallway, which of course, didn't really seem to be moving.

I told him that I did not have any cash to pay him. He waved his hands and told me it wouldn't be a problem, he would drive me to an ATM in the city.

Relieved, I walked briskly alongside him. I smiled at the line at the ATM. They weren't fortunate enough to be selected by this cab driver. They had to wait, where as I was well on my way to my hotel bed.

We left the airport, and I instinctively began walking to the taxi stand, but he said that his cab wasn't at the front. That's odd, I thought.

We walked across the parking lot, further and further away from the taxi stand.

He stopped in front of a beat up, green mini van. This was definitely not a licensed taxi. I suppose the easiest place way to locate and kidnap rich, tired, and disoriented Americans would be at the airport, early in the morning after all the international red eyes get in. I had seen this video of a man who holds out a burlap sack for a baby kangaroo, who instinctively jumps in. I felt a bit like that baby roo, thoughtlessly jumping into the car.

Wordlessly, he grabbed my bags and tossed them in the back and opened the passenger door. He noticed that I had not yet gotten in. "Are we all good?", he asked.

"We are all good," I replied awkwardly. Turns out I would rather risk being kidnapped than risk offending a complete stranger in a foreign country by declining his ride and somehow implying it's not safe.

NEW YEARS

There is only one universal holiday in the world, and that is New Years. New Years is in many ways the only natural holiday. At its most simple, our calendar has intervals called years, and thus we have a first day of each year, hence the holiday New Years. By the simple fact that we measure time, we are predestining ourselves to New Years holiday.

Suffice it to say it, it is wonderful to be in another country on New Years because you know there will be a celebration.

I had arrived in "America del Sur", which was the name of my hostel on New Year's Eve. The hostel had planned a potluck for New Years where patrons could have a meet and greet over international food.

In my room, I meet one of my three suite mates. He was a Brazilian man in his late twenties who pretty much immediately told me that his wife had recently left him and that he was traveling alone. English was not his first language, and he told me this in that matter-of-fact way that you convey language when you don't know enough words for nuance.

We very wrongly assumed that we would be only ones dorky enough to bring food to the potluck. We settled on spaghetti and meatballs for dinner, with my roommate insisting that we add carrots to the red sauce. This ended up being, to my surprise, a good touch.

The tiny hostel kitchen was packed that night.

There were Belgian waffles, made by Belgians; eggs and bacon, made by Brits; Swedish bread cake, made by Swedes; and of course, our spaghetti, made by the Brazilians and myself.

Our spaghetti took an inexplicably long time to prepare, so I missed almost all of the food; there was plenty more dishes than those described above, and about thirty to forty people crammed into the lobby. Fortunately, I did have a chance to try the Swedish bread cake.

Swedish bread cake's name is both deceptive and honest. It's not a cake, even though it has that in the name and it does look like a cake. In truth, it's a sandwich dressed up like a cake, with layers of bread, smoked salmon, and egg salad, topped with ham and citrus.

Frankly, it sounds and looks disgusting, which I assume, is why there was so much left.

"Do you like it?", asked one of the two Swedish girls who had made it. I responded that I loved the cake. "Perfect," she said.

Now I will pause right here to say the awkward truth. Every young man would like to have two attractive young Swedish women to make them bread cake, even if they don't know what bread cake is. And yes, there is a certain allure to being able to say that you spent the night chatting up two Swedish girls while traveling abroad. And yes, these all were all thoughts I had when I asked if they were going to see the fireworks later that night.

I feel no shame in these things.

We walked down to Puerto Madero, which is the upscale modern district of the city. It is small, thin neighborhood set against the banks of the Rio del Plata, with big glass skyscrapers, a distinct contrast to the plaster and stone Parisian designs of the rest of the city. A twenty-something Saudi man, who was walking with us, said that this is the type of city he likes to go to. I remember thinking it looks like how I would imagine Tampa Bay would look.

We counted down to the New Years and drank champagne and wine as the fireworks exploded over the port. Afterwords, an excitable British man asked me if I wanted to go clubbing with his friends in Palermo, which is the trendy part of the city. I declined and instead walked the Swedish girls home.

We stopped on the Plaza del Mayo, which is the central plaza that contains the Pink House, the Argentine version of the White House. I told them about how this plaza once had been filled with clamoring Argentines hoping to get a peak of Eva and Juan Peron. They told me that they were going on a road trip through all of South America and that this was their second month abroad.

I was impressed by this, and we created a group thread on instagram, which I named "Swedish Meatballs". They invited me to go skydiving, but I declined, as not even the allure of two Swedish women and all the bread cake I could eat would get me to jump out of a plane.

SALTA

By the time I had arrived in Salta, I had lost my credit card and was still recovering from a bout of food poisoning in Iguazu Falls. Pre-modern medicine, doctors would often prescribe sickly patients to go to climates with dry air and a high elevation. Salta would fit that bill perfectly. It sits in a desert basin right at the eastern most edge of the Andes mountains. It's quite high, and it's quite dry.

I wandered around Salta, feeling a bit like an old timey tuberculous patient retiring to the American southwest. I had no cell service, no credit cards, and very little cash on hand. Due to persistent stomach issues, I ate very light, mostly bread from a supermarket, though I did indulge in a few empanadas, which Salta is famous for.

Salta is not a large city, and the roads felt quiet and dusty, the sort of place where an errant tumbleweed would not be unexpected. It's not just dust and single story adobe homes though. The churches are candy striped in bright, contrasting pastels, giving them an almost gingerbread house appearance. Since most of the old town in Salta is no taller than two stories, they dominate the cityscape. With nowhere in particular to go, I found myself walking from church to church.

Eventually, my wanderings brought me to the Museum of High Altitude Archeology, which is famous for housing the Children of Llullaillaco.

In 1999, the bodies of three mummified children were found at the summit of Llullaillaco, a volcano about 200 miles west of Salta, near the Chilean border. Due to the extreme dryness of the Atacama Desert and frigid air of the mountain, the mummies have hardly decayed and are considered the some of the best preserved in the world.

The children were the victims of a year long Capacocha ritual that culminated in their abandonment atop Llullaillaco sometime around 1500 AD. The museum only displays one mummy at a time in order to better preserve the specimens. On that day, the museum was displaying El Nino, thus named because he was a young boy thought to be aged around seven years old at the time of his death.

He was housed in a darkened glass display case, which would light up at the press of a button. He had been tied up and wrapped in a red blanket which covered the ropes. He wore a grey tunic, silver bracelets, and had single white feather tied to his forehead. He sat in the fetal position, with his head resting against his knees. Everything from his skin to his hair was perfectly preserved. It looked like he had just drifted off to sleep.

It was a poignant moment. Not one I think I could easily put into words.

You study history. You go to museums, you see golden artifacts in glass boxes, and watch movies about gladiators. But there is always a distance between you and the past. There's an abstraction that makes it tolerable.

But in that room, the past is present. It's sitting the in the room and confronts you with its brutality.

I've always been a fan of history, but in that moment, I was glad to be in the present and to be able to step out of that room and once again, be worlds away from the past.

JUJUY

At 6 am, I packed my bag with bread and sun screen and waited for the tour bus in the hotel lobby to pick me up. Yes, I was going to the Andes, but no, I would not be mountaineering. I would be shuttled from place to place. I would step out to take pictures and then get right back into the bus. I was looking forward to it.

We would be working our way through the Quebrada de Humahuaca, a valley in the Jujuy province of Argentina bordering Bolivia.

Our first stop was Purmamarca, a small town of a little over 2,000, built around the base of the Seven Colored Hill. This hill is the lifeblood of the town as it brings in throngs of tourists. It consists of alternating layers of red, orange, and white sediment, giving the hill a bit of a rainbow effect.

The picture I would take in front of it would later serve as my de facto headshot.

The town itself consisted of a series of short rectangular adobe homes. When I say the town is small, it really is tiny. It took no more than 15 minutes to walk from end to end. I noticed that there were a few hostels among the homes, presumably for hikers who were more adventurous than me.

The Andes mountains fully surrounded the town. Coming from the East Coast, I had never seen mountains like this before. I always imagined mountains to be rocky and distinct. The Andes, at least near Purmamarca, blend together to form massive ridges. They appear to be covered in a layer of sandy top soil, so as a result, they don't look rocky but instead look like giant mounds of coarse reddish brown sand.

But as we continued on, the mountains shook off their layer of top soil and rose up into red rocky giants. The road was dotted every so often with a smattering of single room homes. I assume that officially these collections of buildings were towns, but I did not recall seeing a single person walking around as we drove past.

Humahuaca was different. Compared to the countryside, it was bustling. 10,000 people live there and on that day, the town was holding a festival for Three Kings Day. People clapped and danced as a procession of kids dressed in red ponchos and blue gaucho costumes paraded around town.

There was singing, dancing, and general merriment as I awkwardly stood on the side of the street. I felt a little bit like I was visiting a human zoo. If I was one of those kids, dressed in a baby blue sombrero, forced by my parents to sing and clap up and down the town, I would not have appreciated the added embarrassment of international travelers gawking and taking pictures.

The final stop was Tilcara, a stone fort that dates back 10,000 years. In truth, it's less of a fort and more a collection of stone walls and three pronged cactuses that wind up a hill. At the top, there is a stone pyramid that stands about 20 feet tall and a few stone houses with roofs made out of woven cactus. A group of teenagers were smoking and playing music next to an informational plaque. Some kids go to the mall to hang out. Others go to a UNESCO world heritage site deep in the Andes. To each his own.

On the way back, we passed the same small towns, and I even spotted a farmer working a gravel dirt field. The contrast between the comfort of the air-conditioned bus and the hot Andean sun was not lost on me. I checked the back of my neck to find that I had developed a sunburn. I was not cut out for this part of the world.

 

Read Before Going

The Tango Singer Tomas Eloy Martinez perfectly captures the history and culture of Buenos Aires in this fast paced novel. Bruno Cadogan flies to Buenos Aires to find an elusive tango singer. In his quest, he strays deeper and deeper into the surreal nature of the city. Read this if nothing else.

Buenos Aires: Biography of a City This plainly titled book covers the architectural history of the Paris of South America. Perfect for those who want to know the story behind the sights.

The Argentina Reader A collection of primary source documents, this anthology covers the history of Argentina from its founding to present. You can pick and choose sections to read based on interest.

Ficciones Jorge Luis Borges is the most famous Argentian writer, perhaps the most famous Latin American writer. Read this for a crash course in his surrealism.

Three Other Things

Recoleta Cementary A vast collection of above ground graves in Buenos Aires' trendy Recoleta neighborhood, this cementary is a must do. It takes a while to find, but search out Eva Peron's final resting place.

Caminito I asked my Brazilian hostel mates if they wanted to join me. They said no. I can see why. This is perhaps the most touristy place I've ever been. If you are looking for street art however, this might be the place.

El Ateneo Argentians are avid readers. This former opera house was converted into giant bookstore and was named the world's most beautiful bookstore by National Geographic.
