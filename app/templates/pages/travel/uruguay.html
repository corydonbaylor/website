MONTEVIDEO

On a Sunday night, you can walk around the Old City of Montevideo and not see a single person.

I was walking through Montevideo with Selena and Denise, two middle aged American women that I had met in my hostel. We were going to a restaurant in the old city, and on the walk from our hostel, we did not see more than ten people.

I can't think of a particular reason that Montevideo isn't crowded with tourists. It really is quite wonderful. The southern border of the city terminates with La Rambla, a 13 mile promenade that runs parallel to the Rio de Plata, with its many beaches and palm trees.

In Ciudad Viaje, the buildings have the same pseudo-parisian architecture as Buenos Aires, though on a much smaller scale. In fact, Ciudad Viaje is no more than fourteen city blocks. It feels even smaller when its empty—kind of like being inside Epicot after closing.

Though that's not to say that the buildings have a Disneyland feel to them. Just the opposite. Many of the old buildings are rundown, covered in graffiti, or behind a padlock. Apparently, many people moved out of Ciudad Viaja during an inflation crisis around 2001, and the area has been a perpetual state of about-to-recover since then.

Running through the center of Ciudad Viaja is a chain of three plazas, Plaza Zabala, Plaza Matriz, and Plaza Independicia. Plaza Independica has mishmash of historic buildings around it, including the Palacio Salvo sky scraper, which as far as I can tell serves as the de facto brochure cover for the city. Plaza Matriz is unremarkable save for its well kept McDonalds and cherub fountain.

And Plaza Zabala is famous for its parakeets and for its penis fence. That is correct, it has a penis fence.

Before its grand opening in 1931, Eduardo Andre, the landscape architect of the park, found that he was going to be significantly shortchanged on his fees and in retribution installed a wrought iron fence with poles bent to have an unmistakable resemblance to the male anatomy.

But Montevideo is a famously relaxed city, in a famously relaxed country. So the penis fence stayed.

CHRISTMASTIME IN SOUTH AMERICA

I have never really understood the economics of open air markets. There's heavy foot traffic but hardly anyone buying anything, and those that do buy something could probably get a better deal at a big box store. It's this endless whirlpool of cheap electronics, matte cups, leather wrist bands, and repackaged barbie dolls that appear to be bought, sold, and then resold endlessly every Sunday at the Tristan Narvaja street market in Montevideo.

I cannot imagine that a local sees a display of matte cups and thinks, "This ten dollar cup is a good idea. I should buy it." No, the tourist's wallet provides the suction that keeps the whirlpool spinning.

On that particular Sunday morning, I was the tourist browsing the old dolls, usb fans, and reusable shopping bags on display. It was December 29th, four days after Christmas, and I had been joined once again by Selena and Denise. They had insisted that we go to the market. I had a good idea of what would be there and was not particularly interested in going. But the idea of spending another day walking alone around an empty old town Montevideo convinced me.

And so, we found ourselves walking the length of the market, every now and then feigning interest in the various fruits, yellowed books, and 'hand crafted' goods.

An arcade, consisting of a series of old connected pastel buildings with arched windows and doorways, lined the market on either side of the street. Most of the storefronts were locked up, but on the corner, the sound of live music spilled out of a bar.

Inside felt like a totally different place and time. A three piece band played violin, piano, and cello in the corner as the bartender loudly took drink orders and smashed fruit to make a homemade rum bucket.

The bar was going for an eclectic hipster aesthetic. Overtop the bar hung four different chandeliers, the type that would typically be found hanging above a middle class American dinner table. One chandelier, I am pretty sure, was really just a porch light. Old leather bound books, liquor and wine bottles, and a grey cathode ray Sony TV filled the shelves behind the bar, and on top of the bar, sat pies, quiches and jars filled with pickles and cereal.

The decorations all looked like something that could have been bought at the market outside but were arranged in just the right way to give a sense of authenticity and humor to the establishment.

I, the quintenssital tourist, was taking picture of the band when suddenly the bar erupted in shouts and cheers.

The bartender was yelling "Feliz Navidad!" as he tossed out felt Santa hats into the crowd. I snatched one out of the air and proudly put it on to pose for more photos.

After grabbing a beer, I decided to head to the rooftop, where I found yet another musician, this time playing guitar. I rested my elbows against the lip of the roof's wall and looked over the market.

It was Christmastime in Montevideo.

COLONIA

I typically don't feel lonely when traveling alone. This whole trip in many ways was a testament to that. There is a stronger sense of adventure when you are on your own. Something could go wrong, and it would be up to you to figure it out. That can be a fun feeling. But I did feel lonely at Colonia del Sacramento.

Colonia is a romantic town. Everywhere I turned there appeared to be different couples holding hands and looking wistfully into each other's eyes, soaking it all in. They would go home and talk about what a wonderful time that had spent with each other. I, of course, did not have that.

I wandered the sun soaked cobblestone streets of Colonia alone. I peeked my head into the faded pastel buildings and inquired shopkeeps about their trinkets and toys alone. I climbed the white washed lighthouse and looked out upon the sparkling waters of Rio de la Plata alone.

And when I sat down to get drinks at a sea side bar, I damn near expected the waiter to say, "I am sorry sir, we cannot accommodate a party of one. It upsets the other, more popular, patrons."

I had come to Colonia in part because everyone I had talked to said it was a must do, but also because I was sure that I would get wonderful pictures for my dating apps if I went. There is something inherently romantic about the Ciudades Viajes of South America. In the United States, many old towns grew up to be city centers or at least adjacent to them. There are few that retain the bucolic beauty of their founding.

Colonia has that beauty.

It was founded as a Portuguese outpost in 1680 and would trade between Spanish and Portuguese control twelve times before finally becoming part of Uruguay in 1828. Throughout the constant changing of hands, Colonia never grew up. It retains the same crooked layout, the same cobblestone streets, the same plaster buildings, the same church, and the same lighthouse. You can easily imagine a Portuguese sailor disembarking here in 1688, fully aware that they are at the edge of the world, and retreating into their single story home.

And now it would be the perfect backdrop to a tinder profile picture. Or at least, that was my thought. I found that when I asked people to take my picture, they often times rushed and would get me with my eyes closed. Eventually, I found an American tourist well practiced in the art of picture taking. She took my picture of me sitting by a green wooden door, underneath a winding tree with pink flowers that grew like a vine alongside the brick wall of an old Portuguese house.

This was perfect. I sent it to Megan, a girl I had gone one one date with three weeks prior. We traded texts everyday. It made the trip feel less lonely.

 

ENAMEL PLATES

On advice from Selena and Denise, I took a forty minute cab ride to the middle of Montevideo to pick up enamel plates from Estamaltados SUE. The store itself--despite being featured in a must-do travel article--felt almost shockingly nondescript. The storefront was a single room, separated from the elements by a rolling metal grate. Hundreds of pots, pans, plates, bowls and other various enamel dishes hung along the walls and stacked up throughout the room.

After traveling to an ATM to retrieve cash, I bought a half dozen plates and a tea kettle. They really were nice. They had red rims and specs of red paint across body of the plate. The plates felt sturdy and modern, but still the type of plate that a kid would eat Kraft Mac and Cheese off of. Due to their metallic feel, I surmised that I could not put these in a microwave.

With enamel plates in hand, I ordered a cab back to the hostel. The cab was an old car, painted black with yellow trim and driven by an Italian looking man with white mustache. As we drove back, it began to rain. It was one of those light rains, where the sun peaks through in spots from time to time. The city began bustling with people running into stores and homes, covering their heads with newspapers.

The cab took me down a different route back than the one I came. We went through a residential part of town, filled with single story, plaster homes. The houses were painted pastel colors with white trimmed windows and ceramic tiled roofs. Despite being made from concrete and plaster, the houses looked almost flimsy, though perhaps lived-in is the better word. The lawns, which were no more than ten feet from road to doorstep, were encased in metal pole fencing. There wasn't much grass or greenery, so the rain quickly pooled along the roadside, and people splashed in puddles as they dashed inside. Needless to say, this is the Montevideo that people actually live in.

The driver was singing along to the radio. I wish I could remember which song was playing--though I am not sure I had heard it before or since. Through my limited Spanish, I could tell the singer was a contestant performing for a singing competition. She had a light airy voice, which lent a quality of both hope and mourning to the melody, even as she stumbled across some of the English pronunciations.

It seemed to fit so well--the rain, the town, and this song. Uruguay, like its big brother Argentina, had seen better days and as the rain poured and the woman sang this hopeful song, I felt a thick clump of emotion rise within me. Emotion for a place and time that I had never known and had no claim to even really care for. I wanted the clouds to part and for Uruguay to be a bustling melting pot of Italian and Spanish immigrants, bringing new old-world cuisines, customs, and French architecture into the city once again.

Eventually both the rain and the song ended, and I was back in old town. I paid the cab driver and stepped out back into the tourist approved section of town, which was just as empty as I had left it.

Two Other Things

Mercado del Puerto This historic main market is located steps from the port. You can buy all sorts of meats and groceries here. I unfortunately purchased an incrediblely expensive plate of different meats and sausages that were a little too greasy for my palate.

Cafe Brasilero I happened upon this spot after attempting to get breakfast someplace else. Turns out that this cafe is Uruguay oldest operating business. The churros with choclate sauce were the best I have ever had. Do not miss this.

Read Before Going

Little Indiscretions A fun, fast-paced mystery, Little Indiscretions combines social satire and classic whodunit tropes into an enjoyable if simple book. A great book to read at the beach.

Alive! In 1972, the Uruguayan rugby team crashed deep in the Andes mountains. Follow the harrowing tale of the 34 survivors as they try to reach civilization.
